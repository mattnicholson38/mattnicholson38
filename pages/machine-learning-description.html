Machine Learning and stock selection


Machine Learning algorithms have proliferated widely in recent years, though many of them are not new. Neural Networks and Fuzzy Logic have been around for at least twenty years. Advances in computing, data availability, and not the least their success in predicting what people want to buy, has lead to their proliferation. Several algorithms have been employed here in a simple manner as an interesting means of semi-randomly selecting stocks for the incubators. The implementation is loosely based on the formulation of Nicolas Carbone in Towards Data Science, Find stocks worth buying with Machine Learning.

Each Machine Learning algorithm required training on a significant set of data in order to "learn" which stocks are most likely to go up in the following quarter. 31 quarterly metrics for each stock in the S&P 500 over the 5 years leading up to 2020 were used as the training data. The classification of "success" for the training set was defined as any stock that appreciated more than 5 or 6 percent during a quarter, with the exact threshold varying for new quarters in order to keep the number of stocks selected reasonable. The trained algorithms were run on the key metrics for the quarter preceding the period of performance and the stocks selected were then included in the fund and its performance tracked for that period. For example, quarterly results for 3/31/20 were processed by the trained algorithms to select stocks to hold during the 3 months from 4/1/20 to 6/30/20, and so on. See the wikipedia descriptions for Support Vector Machine and Random Forests or random decision forests for more details.
